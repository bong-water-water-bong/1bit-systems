Public-domain policy

Last updated 2026-04-23

Where we host

1bit.systems infrastructure runs in Canada (our streaming origin is in Toronto; our training pod at RunPod is in the US but is not part of the distribution path). Canadian copyright law determines what we can host and serve.

Canadian cinematographic works have a 50-year-from-publication term that was grandfathered through the 2022-12-30 CUSMA extension. Films published before 1972 and literary works by authors who died before 1972 entered the Canadian public domain before the term extension and remain public domain. That's broader than US copyright for the same material.

Why you might see a red X

Your browser sends a country code in its network metadata when you connect. We check that code against the declared public-domain jurisdictions for each work on the page. If the work is not public domain in your country, we show a small red X alongside its play controls, and a banner at the top of the page.

We do not geo-block. Content plays regardless of where you are. The indicator is informational so you can make your own call about whether reproducing, redistributing, or republishing the work is legal for you.

What our obligation is

We serve the content from Canadian servers under Canadian law, which allows us to distribute any work that has entered the Canadian public domain. That's our position.

Your obligations are yours. If you download, redistribute, or publicly perform a work in a jurisdiction where it remains copyrighted, that is on you. The red X is our best-effort way of making the question visible to you before you do any of those things.

What happens if the law changes

If Canadian law changes to retroactively extend terms for works already in the public domain, we would take affected works offline pending legal review. We do not believe retroactive copyright extension is likely; it would conflict with the CUSMA grandfathering provision and Canadian constitutional doctrine around retrospective legislation.
