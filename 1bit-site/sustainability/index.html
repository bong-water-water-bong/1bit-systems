The problem with traditional streaming

Every play of every track is its own round trip. Spotify / Apple Music / Tidal transcode masters at scale, replicate across continental CDNs, and push ~50–450 MB/hr to the listener for each session. Streaming is roughly 1% of global electricity and rising. The architecture assumes bandwidth is free; it isn't.

What we do differently

A 1.58-bit ternary language model encodes an entire artist's catalog. The model file ships to the listener once and decodes on their own Strix Halo iGPU in real time. Subsequent plays pull zero bytes from our servers.

Data-center reduction at scale

Spotify-scale egress (~30 exabytes/yr) needs dozens of regional CDN nodes, continuous transcode fleets, and thick cross-ocean links. Our model reduces the same listener-hour demand by 95–99% because the catalog ships once and decodes a thousand times from local storage.

Fundamentally different scaling curve. Streaming's bandwidth footprint, done this way, scales with catalog size, not with playback sessions. A 100 M-subscriber 1bit-style service runs on infrastructure that today's services use for a few hundred thousand users.

Client-side savings

  • Longer device lifetime — ternary kernels are DRAM-bandwidth-light (~10× less traffic per token than fp16), so playback doesn't thermal-throttle laptops and drains less battery on phones.
  • Rural / mobile access to lossless — a full catalog downloads once on WiFi and replays infinitely offline. Previously impractical on metered mobile connections.
  • Airplane / subway / cottage — there is no offline mode to enable, because there is no online mode to fall off of. Once the file is on your device, it plays.
