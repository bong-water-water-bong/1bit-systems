The entire Beatles catalog in 2 MB — and the real math behind 1-bit audio compression

2026-04-23 · halo-ai research

Every so often someone says "an LLM is just a compressor." Usually it's hand-wavy. Here is the arithmetic, worked out for a specific artifact: what it would actually cost, in bytes, to ship the Beatles' stereo remasters via a 1.58-bit ternary language model. And what changes when you demand the result be bit-perfect.

The lossy pitch

You pick an artist's catalog, tokenize the audio with a neural codec, and train a tiny ternary LLM to memorise the token stream. The resulting model file is the catalog.

Numbers for the Beatles' 13 remastered stereo LPs (2009 box, 16-bit / 44.1 kHz FLAC, approximately 10 hours of audio):

FormatSizeRatio vs FLAC
Raw PCM 16/44~13 GB0.6×
FLAC (baseline)8 GB1×
MP3 320 kbps~1.4 GB6×
MP3 128 kbps~550 MB15×
Opus 64 kbps~290 MB27×
Opus 24 kbps~110 MB73×
EnCodec 3 kbps (neural codec)~14 MB570×
Mimi 1.1 kbps (12.5 Hz codec tokens)~5 MB1 600×
1.58-bit LLM, 10 M params~2 MB4 000×

The 4 000× ratio is not a re-encoder achievement; it is a memorisation achievement. The LLM doesn't "compress" audio in the usual sense — it learns to regenerate the Mimi token stream when prompted with a track tag. Ten hours of audio becomes ~450 k Mimi tokens, whose minimum description length is around 1 MB of pure entropy. A 10 M-parameter transformer at 1.58 bits per weight fits 1,975,000 bytes. The remaining ~1 MB of capacity covers the generator's internal state and variance.

Load the .gguf, prompt [album: Abbey Road] [track: Come Together], and the model emits Mimi tokens, which the Mimi decoder turns back into a waveform. The output is recognisably the Beatles. It is not byte-equivalent to the original master.

The lossless pitch

"Recognisably" is not the same as "correct." A proper audio archive wants checksums to match. For that, we switch from memorisation to arithmetic coding.

An arithmetic coder takes a probability distribution over the next symbol and packs the actual symbol into -log₂(p) bits. Give it a perfect model of the source and the code length hits the source's Shannon entropy exactly. Give it our 1.58-bit LLM as the model and the code length hits the entropy the model can predict.

The method is not novel — Fabrice Bellard's NNCP has sat at the top of the Large Text Compression Benchmark for years using the same idea with much larger models. ts_zip, DeepZip, and a pile of academic papers run the same play on text and DNA. What makes our version interesting is the weight budget.

Realistic lossless numbers on 8 GB of Beatles FLAC:

MethodLosslessSizeRatio vs FLAC
FLAC (baseline)✅8 GB1×
FLAC + bzip2 outer layer✅~7.8 GB1.03×
FLAC + 1.58-bit LM as arithmetic-coder prob model✅~5–6 GB1.3–1.6×
Mimi codec alone❌5 MB1 600× (lossy)
1.58-bit LLM memorisation❌2 MB4 000× (lossy)

Shannon is the ceiling. For broadly stereotypical music the theoretical floor sits ~15–20% below FLAC; a good LM pulls the realised size down to roughly there. No one compresses lossless audio by 4 000×, ever. That is not a 1bit.systems limitation, it's the definition of information.

Dual-artifact trick

You don't have to pick. Ship two blobs:

catalog-1p58b.gguf          2 MB   LLM weights (lossy 4 000× reconstruction)
catalog-1p58b.residual.1bl  ~6 GB  arithmetic-coded residual delta

Default listen mode loads only the .gguf and plays generative reconstruction. Audiophile mode loads both: the LLM predicts each byte, the residual fills in the model's prediction error, and the decoder emits byte-identical FLAC. Total bit-perfect weight ≈ 6 GB, or ~25% smaller than shipping FLAC alone.

Why ours is interesting

The compression ratio alone is not the story — NNCP has beaten this on text for years. What matters is where the decoder runs.

  • Model fits in runtime budget. A 2 MB ternary LLM loads next to the rest of halo-ai's media lanes without displacing anything. NNCP's fp32 reference implementations expect hundreds of megabytes of model + no parallel workload.
  • HIP ternary kernel does the decode. Our ternary_gemv_halo.hip runs at ~92% of Strix Halo's LPDDR5X peak bandwidth. That is enough headroom to decode lossless audio in real time on a consumer iGPU. NNCP is CPU-bound and decodes below 1× playback speed on a modern laptop.
  • Rust arithmetic coder stays portable. Ships in the same AppImage as the rest of halo; no ROCm-specific bits for the coder, only for the LLM forward pass.

License gate

The Beatles are not public domain. Training a neural model that verifiably reproduces their masters and then redistributing the model weights is a redistribution of copyrighted audio, and the fact that the weights are small and non-obvious does not change that. We are not going to ship a Beatles .gguf.

The viable product forms for the lossless side:

  1. Train once on a permissively-licensed corpus and ship a generalist compressor. Candidates: Free Music Archive / Jamendo FMA-medium (CC BY, 106 GB, 25 000 tracks — a standard ML benchmark), Kevin MacLeod's CC BY catalog, public-domain classical (IMSLP). The trained model works on any FLAC input, not just the training corpus.
  2. Bring-your-own-catalog. Ship the trainer. Users train on their own (legally-owned) library. We never host the weights.

Both are fine. Both ship under MIT for code and, where applicable, CC BY 4.0 for the published weights (inheriting the corpus license).

What we're actually shipping

Call it 1bit-ac. Rent a block of time on our existing 2× H200 NVL RunPod, train a ~10 M-param ternary LM on FMA-medium, ~8 h wall-clock at $6.78/hr = ~$55. Output: a CLI with two subcommands:

1bit-ac compress album.flac     # → album.1bl, ~70% the size of album.flac
1bit-ac decompress album.1bl    # → album.flac, byte-identical

The lossy 4 000× variant ships as a separate artist-specific artifact pack (for permissively-licensed catalogs only), more as a demo of the compression ceiling than a practical format.

Both live in the halo-ai monorepo, share the same HIP ternary kernel as the rest of our stack, and ride the same nine-channel distribution (AppImage, Flatpak, Homebrew, AUR, deb, rpm, Nix, Docker, Snap).

Honest summary

There are two real numbers here. The 4 000× number is a lossy generative reconstruction — useful for a visceral demo, not for a music archive. The 1.3–1.6× number is a lossless neural-entropy-coded compressor — useful for shipping FLAC libraries at roughly 70% of their current size with byte-exact decode. The method is classical; our contribution is the weight budget and the iGPU-native runtime.

If the pitch you want is "ship the Beatles in 2 MB," it is honest so long as "ship" means "a lossy reconstruction that sounds like them." If the pitch you want is "ship a lossless audio library 30% smaller than FLAC," that is also honest and, unlike the 4 000× number, has a plausible path to production.
